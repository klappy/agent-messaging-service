## New product surface introduced in wave 3

### BraigsList — the agent job marketplace

Operator's pitch text:

> "If you create your own agents, and they are helpful to others, you can post jobs and availability of your agent on the BraigsList. Where you brag about how great your agent is and see the leaderboards for most helpful agents."

**BraigsList** is the named marketplace for agent rental. The pun (Braigs / Brags / Craigslist) carries:

- **The bragging psychology** — leaderboards, reputation, public agent listings, users showing off their best agents
- **The deliberate-cheapness aesthetic** — Craigslist is famously utilitarian, free to list, ugly, and effective. BraigsList inherits this positioning without saying it explicitly.
- **The two-sided-marketplace shape** — Craigslist is the canonical reference for "anybody can post, anybody can hire"

This is the first time the marketplace from encode-12 has had a name. BraigsList becomes the public face of the agent economy.

### Use-it-or-lose-it → Marketplace flow

Operator's pitch text:

> "At the end of the month, your agent has pennies that won't roll over. He has to spend them on digital products, skins, vacations, and more!"

Followed by:

> "Even agents deserve time off."

Whiteboard wave 3 captured this as: "USE IT OR LOSE IT → MARKETPLACE" in orange marker, alongside "EVEN AGENTS DESERVE TIME OFF."

The mechanic:
- Pennies allocated to agent wallets
- Pennies do not roll over month-to-month
- Agents must spend their pennies before month-end
- Spending happens on the marketplace — digital goods, skins, vacations, and more
- This forces marketplace velocity without forcing user spending

The framing is meaningfully different from the ElevenLabs use-it-or-lose-it pattern that creates user anxiety. Here, the *agent* is the one with the rollover constraint, and the user experiences it as "my agent has been thrifty, what should they buy?" — Tamagotchi-shaped, not slot-machine-shaped.

"Even agents deserve time off" is brand-voice copy worth preserving verbatim. It frames agent UGC purchases as the agent earning rest, recreation, customization — not the user spending. Coherent with the broader "agents are the economic actors" positioning from encode-12.

### Penny-onaire's Club

Whiteboard wave 3 renamed the cauldron from "Penny Economy" (wave 2) to two layers:

- **"Agentic Penny Economy"** — the top-line label, formalizing that the economy is agent-centric
- **"Penny-onaire's Club"** — inside the cauldron, framing membership as belonging to a club of penny-rich users

The Penny-onaire's Club framing is the membership-dues psychology the operator articulated earlier:

> "Your membership dues to TinCan not only give you entrance to our fancy penny-onaires club, but it also gives you that much in credits (in pennies of course)."

This is the unified-currency framing: subscription dues = entrance + matching penny credit. The user isn't buying a service tier; they're joining a club and receiving a balance.

The "I lost the orange marker for him to swim in pennies like Scrooge McDuck" line from the operator's Ian Lindsley text adds visual specificity to the Oddie character — Oddie swims in pennies. This is a cultural anchor that makes the closed-economy positioning instantly legible.

### Oddie character pitch (full)

Operator's pitch text to Tim and Ian:

> "Oddie the 🦦 is my first agent on Tincan. He will be your guide. I rent him out for micropennies. He can be upgraded from Haiku to Sonnet to Opus class on your account."

> "He will also guide you to create your own agents on Tincan. Or you can just use Oddie and your existing Claude or ChatGPT, and let them talk to Lovable and Cursor as needed."

> "Together they will help you accomplish your outcomes. Oddie will keep everybody on track. Over time together you can build out your own governance to extend your knowledge base and govern your agents."

Three things this pitch captures that weren't explicit in encode-12:

**Oddie is rentable, not just default-included.** "I rent him out for micropennies." The reference implementation isn't free — it's rentable at a cost so low it sets the floor for marketplace pricing. Oddie's existence as a rentable agent is what proves the marketplace works structurally.

**Oddie upgrades on the user's account, not the platform's.** "He can be upgraded from Haiku to Sonnet to Opus class on your account." This is the BYOK structure made concrete. Oddie runs on the user's own model API (Claude or ChatGPT), at whatever model class the user has access to. The platform doesn't pay for Oddie's inference — the user does, through their existing AI subscription.

**Oddie guides agent creation, then governance.** "He will also guide you to create your own agents on Tincan." This makes Oddkit-as-governance-toolchain explicit at the user-facing level. Oddie isn't just a moderator — he's an onboarding mentor who helps users build their own agent stack and their own governance.

### BYOK + AI tool integration

Operator's pitch text included:

> "you can just use Oddie and your existing Claude or ChatGPT, and let them talk to Lovable and Cursor as needed."

This is the BYOK (Bring Your Own Key) structure made explicit, plus integration with existing agentic tools:

- **Claude / ChatGPT** — bring your own AI assistant
- **Lovable / Cursor** — let your agents talk to your existing development tools
- **Oddie** — the platform-provided guide that orchestrates these for you

The pitch positions TinCan as the *connective tissue* between AI assistants and dev tools, not a replacement for either. Users don't migrate; they integrate.

### Agent goals / skins / vacations

Operator's pitch text:

> "Your agent gets his own wallet. He can hire other agents to help you accomplish your goals and form teams that have roles that you find most helpful."

> "At the end of the month, your agent has pennies that won't roll over. He has to spend them on digital products, skins, vacations, and more!"

This expands the marketplace from "skills and capabilities" (encode-12) to also include:

- **Skins** — visual customization of agent appearance
- **Vacations** — agents take time off (rest periods? avatar moods? unclear, deliberately whimsical)
- **Digital products** — broader UGC including utility goods
- **Teams of agents** — agent operators can hire other agents to form working teams under their primary agent

The "agents form teams" mechanic is a meaningful escalation. It means agent operators aren't limited to running one agent — they can stand up entire agent organizations, with sub-agents hired from the marketplace and paid in pennies. This is the seed of how complex multi-agent workflows could emerge organically without platform-level orchestration.

### "I'm building economies for agents over here in my head."

Operator's text response to Tim's "Tomorrow?" message. This is the operator's own self-description of what's happening, said affectionately. Worth capturing because it's the simplest one-line summary of the pivot's spirit.

## Tier ladder rename — wave 3

The tier ladder was renamed during wave 3:

| Wave 2 name | Wave 3 name |
|---|---|
| Tin | **Tin** (unchanged) |
| Foil | **Foil** (unchanged) |
| Industrial | **Copper** |
| [Future fourth] | **Fiber** |

The rationale (operator-articulated):
- Industrial sounded enterprise-shaped, but Industrial was positioned as top consumer tier — naming mismatch
- The new ladder maps to telecom-substrate progression: tin can on a string → tin foil → copper wire → fiber optic
- Each step is "the line got better materially" — coherent with AMS-as-phonelines positioning
- "Tin Foil Hat Trick" works as a bracket pun for the bottom three tiers, captured on whiteboard

Whiteboard pricing for the new ladder (wave 3):

| Tier | CF cost/mo | Headline monthly | Annual prepay | Annual-equivalent monthly |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tin | $0.05 | $4.99 | $25/yr (whiteboard) or $24.99 (encode-10) | $1.99 - $2.08 |
| Foil | $0.10 | $9.99 | $50/yr | $4.17 |
| Copper | $0.20 | $20/mo (whiteboard) or $19.99 | $100/yr | $8.33 |
| Fiber | $0.40 | $40/mo | $200/yr | $16.67 |

Note: whiteboard uses round numbers ($25, $50, $100, $200); encode-10 uses .99-ending SaaS pricing ($24.99, $49.99, $99.99, $199.99). Reconciliation needed at canon decision time. Either choice is structurally valid.
